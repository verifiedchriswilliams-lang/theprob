I want to skip the normal format today and just talk to you directly.

I built The Prob to answer a question that has been bothering me for a while. Prediction markets aggregate information from thousands of people putting real money on outcomes. They are, in theory, the closest thing we have to a truth machine. So here is the question: can you actually trade them profitably? Or is the crowd already right, and you are just paying to feel smart?

I do not know the answer. So I built a system to find out.

Starting today, I am running three separate paper trading strategies simultaneously on The Prob. Same $1,000 starting balance each. Same markets. Same clock. No retroactive edits. Whatever happens, happens in public.

Model A
The Crowd
Bet YES when 65%+ of money agrees. Bet NO when 35% or less. Follow conviction. Never fade a clear signal.
Model B
The Contrarian
Always bet NO on longshots priced 30% or below. Crowds systematically overprice unlikely events. Sell the ticket.
Model C
The Arb
Find markets where Polymarket and Kalshi disagree by 10+ points. Bet the lagging platform. Two liquid crowds cannot both be right.

Each model represents a real thesis about how prediction markets work. Model A says the crowd is smart and you should trust it. Model B says the crowd is irrational about longshots (there is actually solid research behind this, called longshot bias). Model C says when two independent platforms price the same event very differently, someone is wrong and you can profit from the gap.

I am genuinely not sure which one wins. That is the honest answer. If I knew, I would just run that one.

The experiment runs 30 days. Every trade is logged in real time. Every rule is published. No retroactive changes. You can watch the whole thing live at theprobnewsletter.com/portfolio.html.
